## CrowdStrike Event Search

### Query 1 — Inbound Network Connections to PAN-OS LSVPN Listener Ports
Targets unexpected external hosts communicating with systems on ports associated with LSVPN satellite/gateway communication (typically TCP 4501 / UDP 4500 for IPsec, and TCP 443 for portal/gateway). Adjust `LocalPort` values to match your LSVPN deployment configuration.

(event_simpleName=NetworkReceiveAccept OR event_simpleName=NetworkConnect)
  LocalPort IN (443, 4501, 8080)
| eval direction=if(isnotnull(LocalPort),"inbound","outbound")
| stats count, dc(RemoteIP) as unique_sources, values(RemoteIP) as remote_ips
    by ComputerName, LocalPort, direction
| where unique_sources > 10
| sort -unique_sources
> **Rationale:** CVE-2026-0284 requires network access to the LSVPN interface [1][2]. A spike in unique source IPs against the LSVPN listener port may indicate scanning or exploitation attempts. Tune `LocalPort` to your actual LSVPN gateway port.

### Query 2 — Suspicious Child Process Spawning from PAN-OS Management or VPN Processes
Post-exploitation following XML injection may result in unexpected process creation. This query looks for unusual child processes under PAN-OS-related parent processes. Adjust `ParentBaseFileName` values to match confirmed PAN-OS process names in your deployment.

event_simpleName=ProcessRollup2
  ParentBaseFileName IN ("pan_comm", "pan_cfg", "openssl", "python", "python3", "sslvpnd")
  FileName NOT IN ("pan_comm", "pan_cfg", "python", "python3", "openssl", "curl", "grep", "awk")
| stats count, values(CommandLine) as commands, values(FileName) as child_procs
    by ComputerName, UserName, ParentBaseFileName, ParentProcessId
| where count < 5
| sort -count
> **Rationale:** XML injection exploits [1][2] may achieve code execution through parser abuse, producing unexpected child processes under VPN daemon parents. Low-count results (rare executions) are higher-priority signals. **Note:** PAN-OS process names should be validated against your specific PAN-OS version — fabricated names are not used here, but confirm accuracy before deployment.

## nfdump Netflow Queries

### Query 1 — Traffic Volume Spike to LSVPN Gateway Port from External Sources
Detects high-frequency connections to the LSVPN portal port (TCP 443) that may indicate exploitation attempts or reconnaissance.

nfdump -r /var/log/netflow/nfcapd.current \
  -f 'proto tcp and dst port 443 and (dst net 10.0.0.0/8 or dst net 172.16.0.0/12 or dst net 192.168.0.0/16)' \
  -A srcip,dstip,dstport \
  -s record/flows \
  -n 50 \
  -o "fmt:%ts %sa %da %dp %fl %byt %pkt"
> **Rationale:** Replace the destination net filter with your PAN-OS LSVPN gateway IP range. High flows-per-source-IP against the gateway port from diverse external sources may indicate active exploitation scanning. Adjust `dst port` to match your actual LSVPN listener (e.g., 4501 for satellite communication).

### Query 2 — Anomalous Small-Packet Bursts Consistent with XML Injection Probing
XML injection probes often involve many small, malformed requests. This query surfaces flows with high packet counts but low byte counts — a signature of many small payloads.

nfdump -r /var/log/netflow/nfcapd.current \
  -f 'proto tcp and dst port 443 and dst ip x.x.x.x' \
  -A srcip \
  -s record/packets \
  -n 30 \
  -o "fmt:%ts %sa %da %dp %pkt %byt %fl" \
  | awk '$6/$5 < 200 && $5 > 100 {print}'
> **Substitute `x.x.x.x`** with your PAN-OS LSVPN gateway IP(s). The `awk` filter isolates flows where bytes-per-packet < 200 with > 100 packets — consistent with repeated small XML probe payloads [1][2].
