# Security Advisory: Open WebUI Access Control Vulnerability

> **DRAFT FOR ANALYST REVIEW** — This advisory is a proposed draft. All technical details, timelines, and recommendations should be validated by a qualified security analyst before distribution.

---

## Executive Summary

A significant security weakness has been identified in Open WebUI, a popular web-based interface used to interact with AI language models. The vulnerability allows an attacker to impersonate another legitimate user within the application, effectively bypassing the system's identity checks. This issue carries a **HIGH** severity rating [1] and affects all versions of the software prior to version 0.10.0 [2]. At this time, the recommended action is to monitor the situation and plan for an upgrade to version 0.10.0 or later. No immediate emergency patch deployment is required based on current priority assessment, but teams should be prepared to act quickly if exploitation activity is observed.

---

## Business Impact

If this vulnerability is exploited, a malicious actor — who may already have limited access to the environment — could take on the identity of another user, including potentially privileged accounts such as administrators. This could lead to:

- **Unauthorized access to sensitive data**: An attacker impersonating a legitimate user could view, export, or manipulate data they are not permitted to access, creating potential **data breach exposure**.
- **Regulatory and compliance risk**: Unauthorized access to personal or confidential data processed through AI tools may trigger obligations under data protection regulations (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA, CCPA), including breach notification requirements.
- **Loss of trust and integrity**: If AI-assisted workflows are compromised, the integrity of outputs and decisions made using those tools cannot be guaranteed, potentially affecting business operations that rely on them.
- **Reputational damage**: A confirmed breach involving user impersonation in an internal AI tool could damage confidence among customers, partners, and regulators.

The exploit requires a crafted, deliberate request [1], meaning this is not a trivial or accidental exposure — a motivated attacker with some level of access to the application could feasibly carry it out.

---

## Affected Systems

- **Product**: Open WebUI
- **Affected Versions**: All versions **below 0.10.0** [2]
- **Fixed Version**: Open WebUI **0.10.0 and later** [2][3]

Any internal deployment, cloud-hosted instance, or developer environment running an older version of Open WebUI should be considered potentially at risk.

---

## Recommended Action

Management is asked to **approve and communicate the following actions** to the relevant IT and engineering teams:

1. **Inventory**: Direct teams to identify all instances of Open WebUI deployed across the organization (including development, test, and production environments) within the next **5 business days**.

2. **Plan Upgrade**: Schedule an upgrade to Open WebUI version 0.10.0 or later [3] within the remediation window outlined below. This is the vendor-confirmed fix for this vulnerability [2].

3. **Access Review**: In parallel, request a review of who has access to any Open WebUI instance to ensure that exposure is limited and that no unusual activity has occurred.

4. **Monitoring**: Until the upgrade is completed, ensure that logging and alerting is in place for the affected systems so that any suspicious impersonation attempts can be detected promptly.

5. **Communicate**: Notify relevant teams (IT, DevOps, AI/ML platform owners) of the requirement to remediate and set clear accountability for completion.

> ⚠️ **Note on Severity Discrepancy**: Analysts should be aware that this advisory reflects a CVSS score of **8.0 (HIGH)** [1]. The priority scoring system has assigned this vulnerability a relatively low urgency score of **20 (MONITOR tier)**. This discrepancy between the HIGH technical severity and the lower operational priority score warrants analyst review — the lower priority score may reflect limited evidence of active exploitation or deployment prevalence at this time, but the underlying technical risk remains HIGH [1][2]. Analysts should reassess if exploitation evidence emerges.
