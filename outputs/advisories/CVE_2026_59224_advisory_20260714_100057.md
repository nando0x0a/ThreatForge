# Security Advisory: Open WebUI Access Control Vulnerability
**CVE-2026-59224 | Priority Tier: MONITOR | Severity: HIGH**
*This advisory is a proposed draft for analyst review prior to distribution.*

---

## Executive Summary

A security weakness has been identified in Open WebUI, a popular interface used to interact with AI language models, affecting all versions prior to 0.10.0. The vulnerability allows an attacker to impersonate other users of the system by exploiting a flaw in how the application handles user identity when connecting to its terminal feature. Rated as High severity [1], this issue could allow unauthorised individuals to act as legitimate users without their credentials. While the priority assessment places this in a **MONITOR** tier — meaning immediate emergency action is not required — teams running affected versions should plan to apply the available fix in an orderly and timely fashion.

---

## Business Impact

If left unaddressed, this vulnerability could result in:

- **Unauthorised data access:** An attacker who can send crafted requests to your Open WebUI deployment could impersonate any other user, potentially accessing their conversations, configurations, connected data sources, or AI-generated outputs.
- **Integrity risk:** Actions taken under a falsely assumed user identity (such as modifying settings or submitting queries) could be attributed to the wrong person, undermining accountability and audit trails.
- **Regulatory and compliance exposure:** If Open WebUI is used in environments subject to data protection regulations (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA), unauthorised access to user sessions may constitute a reportable incident, depending on what data is accessible through the tool.
- **Reputational risk:** Discovery of a successful impersonation attack — particularly in environments where AI tools handle sensitive business queries — could erode trust internally and externally.

*Note: Exploitation requires the ability to send crafted HTTP requests to the Open WebUI instance. Publicly internet-exposed deployments carry meaningfully higher risk than those restricted to internal networks.*

---

## Affected Systems

| Product | Affected Versions | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Open WebUI | All versions **below 0.10.0** | Vulnerable [1] |
| Open WebUI | Version **0.10.0 and later** | Patched [2][3] |

**Action required only if your organisation is running Open WebUI versions older than 0.10.0.** If you are unsure which version is deployed, please ask your IT or platform team to confirm.

---

## Recommended Action

Management should authorise and communicate the following:

1. **Inventory (Within 3 business days):** Direct IT/platform teams to identify all internal and externally facing deployments of Open WebUI and confirm their current version.

2. **Upgrade authorisation (Within 7 business days):** Approve an upgrade of all affected Open WebUI instances to version 0.10.0 or later. A fix has been developed and is available [2][3].

3. **Access restriction (Immediate, if upgrade is delayed):** If any Open WebUI deployment is accessible from the public internet and cannot be upgraded promptly, IT teams should be directed to restrict access to trusted network ranges or VPN until patching is complete.

4. **Review of access logs:** Ask security teams to review available logs for any anomalous user activity on affected systems over the past 30 days to determine whether exploitation may have already occurred.

---

## Timeline

Based on the **MONITOR** priority tier, the following remediation schedule is recommended:

| Milestone | Target Timeframe |
|---|---|
| Confirm affected systems inventory | Within **3 business days** |
| Apply patch (upgrade to v0.10.0+) | Within **14 calendar days** |
| Confirm remediation and close tracking | Within **21 calendar days** |
| Log review for potential prior exploitation | Within **7 business days** |

This is not a zero-day emergency requiring immediate out-of-hours response; however, given the High severity rating [1] and the fact that this vulnerability is only 3 days old with a patch already publicly available [2], delaying remediation beyond the windows above is not advisable. Early patching reduces the window for opportunistic exploitation as awareness of this vulnerability grows.
